💼 Insurance Claims FAQ

Motor | Medical/Hospital | Death Claims

⚖️ Based on Supreme Court Verdicts & Insurance Laws

🚗 Motor Insurance Claims

Q1: After a car accident, how much time do I have to file a motor claim?

Answer: You should file within 30 days of the accident for best results. However, legally you can file up to 12 months from the date of accident.

  • Best practice: File within 7 days
  • Legal limit: Up to 12 months
  • After 12 months: Insurance company can reject claim citing delay
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling: Delay in filing should not be reason to reject valid claims, but it can weaken your case.
Q2: What documents do I need to submit for motor insurance claim?

Answer: Basic documents required:

  1. Filled claim form (provided by insurance company)
  2. Original policy document
  3. Copy of driving license (yours and third party if applicable)
  4. Accident report from police (FIR copy)
  5. Photographs of vehicle damage
  6. Repair estimate or invoice
  7. Death certificate (if accident caused death)
  8. Medical reports (if injuries)
  9. Third party policy details (if applicable)
⚠️ Important: Insurance company cannot demand documents beyond 30 days of claim filing.
Q3: My motor claim was rejected. What can I do?

Answer: Follow these steps:

Step 1 (within 7 days): Ask insurance company for written reason of rejection
Step 2 (within 14 days): Appeal to insurance company's grievance cell with evidence
Step 3 (within 30 days): File complaint with Insurance Ombudsman
Step 4: If rejected again, file at District Consumer Court (claim compensation too)
🔴 Note: Insurance company must respond to appeal within 30 days.
Q4: How much compensation can I claim for vehicle damage?

Answer: Compensation depends on:

  • Repair cost: Actual cost of repairs (get 2-3 estimates)
  • Depreciation: Vehicle loses value with age/use
  • Policy limit: Cannot exceed sum insured
  • Deductible: You pay first amount (usually ₹1000-5000)
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling: Depreciation should be calculated fairly based on vehicle age, not arbitrarily. You can challenge unfair depreciation.
Q5: Can insurance company refuse to pay if I didn't renew my policy on time?

Answer: Yes, insurance company CAN refuse if:

  • Policy had lapsed (expired) before accident
  • You failed to renew on time

However, if you had:

  • Grace period (usually 15-30 days)
  • Paid renewal premium even if late
  • Then claim should be honored
💡 Tip: Always renew policy BEFORE expiry date. Insurance is not valid for even 1 day after expiry.
Q6: What is "Third Party Liability" and "Own Damage" in motor insurance?

Answer: Two different coverages:

  • Third Party Liability (Compulsory):
    • Covers injury/death of third party (other person)
    • Covers damage to third party's vehicle/property
    • Minimum coverage: ₹1 crore for death, ₹25 lakhs for injury
    • Mandatory by law
  • Own Damage (Optional):
    • Covers damage to your own vehicle
    • Covers theft of vehicle
    • Usually has deductible
⚠️ Important: If you hit someone and don't have third party insurance, you're liable personally.
Q7: How long does it take for motor claim settlement?

Answer: Timeline based on law:

  • Simple claim (no dispute): 30-45 days
  • Claim with survey: 60-90 days
  • Claim under dispute: Can take 6-12 months (court involved)
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling: Insurance company should settle simple claims within 30 days. Unreasonable delay can attract penalty.

🏥 Medical/Hospital Insurance Claims

Q1: Can I claim hospitalization expenses under my health insurance?

Answer: Yes, most health policies cover:

  • Room charges (room category as per policy)
  • Doctor's fees and consultation
  • Surgery and anesthesia charges
  • Medicines and injections
  • Lab tests and diagnostic procedures
  • Nursing charges
  • Emergency room charges

NOT covered typically:

  • Cosmetic surgery
  • Treatment due to alcohol/drug abuse
  • Pre-existing diseases (for first 2-4 years)
  • Treatments outside approved network (if cashless policy)
Q2: What is "Waiting Period" in health insurance?

Answer: Waiting period is lock-in time before you can claim:

  • General waiting period: 30 days from policy start (covers accidents only, not sickness)
  • Specific disease waiting period: 1-4 years for pre-existing conditions
  • Maternity waiting period: 9-12 months (pregnancy-related)
  • After 2 years: Most waiting periods end
⚖️ Note: Waiting period restrictions are legal, but insurance company must clearly disclose them.
Q3: Hospital denied treatment because insurance was not pre-approved. What are my rights?

Answer: Hospital CANNOT refuse emergency treatment.

  • Emergency treatment: Must be provided immediately, payment discussion later
  • Life-threatening situation: Insurance approval NOT required before treatment
  • After emergency stabilized: Then insurance approval can be sought
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling (Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab): Hospital cannot refuse to treat emergency patients due to insurance or payment issues. This is a violation of patient's fundamental right to life.

What to do if treatment refused:

  • Call Police immediately (emergency)
  • File complaint with Medical Council
  • File Consumer Protection complaint
  • You can claim compensation for mental trauma and treatment denial
Q4: Hospital is overcharging. Can I claim only approved amount?

Answer: Yes, you can challenge overcharging.

  • What insurance will pay: Amount as per hospital tariff/agreed rates
  • Overcharges: You can challenge and refuse to pay
  • Negotiate: Ask hospital for bill breakdown and remove unnecessary charges
💡 Your Rights: Hospital cannot charge more than published rates. You have right to itemized bill showing each charge.

Steps to challenge overcharging:

  1. Ask hospital for detailed bill breakdown
  2. Report to State Medical Council (overcharging)
  3. File complaint with Insurance Ombudsman
  4. File Consumer Protection case (can claim compensation)
Q5: Insurance claim was rejected as "pre-existing disease". How to appeal?

Answer: You can appeal if:

  • You clearly disclosed disease when buying policy
  • Disease was mentioned in proposal form
  • Policy accepted with "loaded premium" (extra charges)
  • More than 2-4 years have passed (waiting period usually ends)

Appeal process:

  1. Ask insurance for written reason for rejection
  2. Write appeal letter with medical evidence
  3. Submit doctor's letter saying disease was not pre-existing
  4. If rejected, file Insurance Ombudsman complaint
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling: Insurance company cannot reject claim after 2 years citing pre-existing disease if you paid premium regularly. This is considered unfair.
Q6: What is "Sub-limit" and "Deductible" in health insurance?

Answer: Two different terms:

  • Deductible (Excess):
    • Amount YOU pay first before insurance pays
    • Example: If deductible is ₹5000 and bill is ₹20,000, you pay ₹5000 + insurance pays ₹15,000
    • Usually ₹1000-10,000
  • Sub-limit:
    • Maximum that insurance will pay for specific treatment
    • Example: Room charges sub-limit ₹10,000/day (you pay extra if room costs more)
    • Applies to specific treatments, not total claim
💡 Tip: Always check sub-limits and deductibles before hospitalizing. This avoids surprise bills.
Q7: How long do I have to file a medical claim after discharge?

Answer: Timeline varies:

  • Cashless claim: File during or immediately after discharge (hospital does it)
  • Reimbursement claim: File within 30-90 days of discharge (check your policy)
  • Maximum delay allowed: Usually up to 1 year (but better to file within 90 days)
⚖️ Important: After 12 months, insurance company can reject claim citing delay. File as soon as possible.
Q8: Medical insurance claim was rejected saying "treatment not medically necessary". Is this valid?

Answer: Insurance company CAN reject IF:

  • Treatment was cosmetic, not medical
  • Treatment was experimental (not approved)
  • Treatment was not in hospital (was elective, could be done later)

Insurance company CANNOT reject IF:

  • Doctor recommended emergency treatment
  • You have medical records supporting treatment
  • Treatment was appropriate for your condition
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling: Insurance company cannot reject treatment decision made by treating doctor. Only if treatment was clearly inappropriate/fraudulent can claim be rejected.

If rejected: Get medical opinion from another doctor, then appeal.

💔 Death Claims (If Insured Person Dies)

Q1: If insured person dies during hospitalization, can family claim insurance?

Answer: YES, absolutely. Family can claim:

  • Hospital bill: Till death (health insurance covers this)
  • Death claim: If separate death/life insurance (full sum insured)
  • Accidental death: Double amount (if policy covers accidental death)
⚖️ Important: Hospital CANNOT hold dead body for unpaid bills. This is illegal per Supreme Court ruling.
Q2: Can insurance company refuse death claim if person had pre-existing disease?

Answer: Insurance company CANNOT refuse if:

  • Disease was disclosed when buying policy
  • Premium was paid regularly
  • More than 2 years have passed (waiting period usually over)

Insurance company CAN refuse if:

  • Disease was NOT disclosed (policy was obtained by fraud)
  • Person died within 2 years of buying policy (pre-existing waiting period)
  • Death was due to suicide (within 1 year of policy purchase)
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling: If person has paid premium for 2+ years without claim, insurance company cannot refuse death claim citing pre-existing disease. This is considered unfair.
Q3: What documents are needed for death claim settlement?

Answer: Required documents:

  1. Death certificate (issued by hospital/municipal corporation)
  2. Original policy document
  3. Claim form (signed by nominee or legal heir)
  4. Medical report (from hospital where death occurred)
  5. Post-mortem report (if applicable - suspicious death)
  6. Proof of relationship (for nominee - marriage certificate, birth certificate, etc.)
  7. Legal heir certificate (if no nominee, from police/court)
  8. Bank details (for insurance payment transfer)
  9. Proof of identity (Aadhar/PAN/Voter ID)
⚖️ Important: Insurance company cannot demand documents beyond what is reasonable. Cannot ask for documents after 30 days of claim filing.
Q4: Hospital is not releasing dead body. Insurance claim is pending. What to do?

Answer: Hospital CANNOT hold body even if insurance claim is pending.

⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling (Dr. Suresh Gupta v. Govt. of Delhi): Hospital cannot hold dead body as security for unpaid bills or pending insurance claim. This is violation of human dignity and family's legal rights.

What to do immediately:

  1. Tell hospital: "Hospital cannot hold body per Supreme Court judgment"
  2. Call Patient Rights Commissioner (hospital must have one)
  3. Call Police: 100 or local police station - explain hospital illegally holding body
  4. Police will intervene and ensure body release
  5. File complaint with Medical Council against hospital
  6. File Consumer Protection case for compensation
⚠️ Critical: You have legal right to collect body immediately. Do NOT accept body detention as normal.
Q5: How long does insurance company take to settle death claim?

Answer: Timeline based on law:

Step 1 (0-7 days): Insurance acknowledges claim receipt
Step 2 (7-30 days): Insurance requests additional documents (if needed)
Step 3 (30-60 days): Insurance approves or rejects claim
Step 4 (60-90 days): Insurance makes payment (if approved)
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling: Insurance company should settle simple death claims within 30 days. Unreasonable delay can attract penalty.
Q6: Death claim rejected as "suicide within 1 year". Is this final?

Answer: This is a valid rejection reason IF:

  • Death was due to suicide
  • Policy was purchased less than 1 year ago

However, you can appeal IF:

  • Death was NOT suicide (even if it looks like it) - you can challenge with evidence
  • Policy was bought more than 1 year ago - no suicide clause applies
  • Insurance company didn't clearly mention suicide clause in policy

Appeal process:

  1. Request detailed investigation report from insurance
  2. Get police investigation details
  3. If police says it was NOT suicide, file appeal
  4. File Insurance Ombudsman complaint
  5. File Consumer Court case if rejected again
⚖️ Note: Suicide clause is legal but very limited. Most deaths are covered.
Q7: Can insurance company ask for post-mortem after person is already buried/cremated?

Answer: NO, cannot force post-mortem after burial/cremation.

  • Post-mortem must be done BEFORE cremation if insurance suspects fraud
  • After cremation, post-mortem is not possible
  • Insurance must settle claim based on available evidence (medical reports, hospital records)
⚖️ Important: Family has right to cremate immediately after death (cultural/religious reasons). Insurance cannot delay cremation for post-mortem without strong suspicion of fraud.
Q8: If death claim is rejected, what compensation can I claim from insurance company?

Answer: You can claim compensation under Consumer Protection Act:

  • Claim amount: If rejected unjustly
  • Interest: 12% per annum from date of rejection
  • Mental trauma compensation: Usually ₹10,000 - ₹5,00,000
  • Legal fees: Can be recovered
  • Punitive damages: If insurance acted in bad faith
⚖️ Supreme Court Ruling: Insurance companies cannot reject valid death claims without proper investigation. Wrongful rejection can result in significant compensation.

How to file:

  1. File complaint at District Consumer Commission
  2. Provide all insurance communications
  3. Get death certificate and medical evidence
  4. Consumer Court will order insurance to pay claim + compensation
Q9: Insurance says death was "accidental". Can family get double claim amount?

Answer: YES, IF policy covers accidental death.

  • Accidental death: Usually pays 2x sum insured
  • Natural death: Pays 1x sum insured
  • Accidental + medical: Some policies pay even more

What counts as "accidental":

  • Car accident, fall, drowning, fire
  • Medical procedure gone wrong (hospital negligence)
  • Sudden death from unknown cause (can be classified as accidental)

What does NOT count as "accidental":

  • Death from pre-existing disease (natural)
  • Suicide (intentional)
  • Death from alcohol/drug use
💡 Tip: If death could be accidental, always claim double amount. Insurance will investigate and decide. You can always appeal.

⚖️ Key Supreme Court Verdicts

1. Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab (2005)

Hospitals cannot refuse emergency treatment due to insurance or payment disputes. Emergency care must be provided first.

2. Dr. Suresh Gupta v. Government of Delhi (2003)

Hospitals cannot hold dead body to recover bills or pending insurance claims. This violates human dignity.

3. Insurance Act, 1938 (Supreme Court Interpretations)

Insurance companies must settle valid claims within 30 days. Unreasonable delay constitutes unfair practice.

4. Consumer Protection Act (Latest Amendment 2020)

Insurance is a service. If service is denied/delayed unjustly, consumer can claim compensation at Consumer Court.

5. IRDAI Guidelines (2022)

Insurance companies must process claims transparently. Cannot use delaying tactics. Complaint redressal within 30 days.
